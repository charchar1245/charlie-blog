My First Steps Into Machine Learning

Two weeks ago, my Algorithms professor recommended that I explore machine learning and AI. Before that moment I had almost no experience — just a vague understanding that it was important, fast-growing, and deeply technical. But once I started learning, everything changed.

Building Foundational Knowledge

I began with Andrew Ng’s “Machine Learning Specialization.” His ability to break down complex mathematical ideas into intuitive pieces made machine learning feel approachable. Concepts like gradient descent, cost functions, decision boundaries, and overfitting stopped being abstract — they finally made sense.

Seeing Neural Networks Visually

After the theory, I needed intuition. Coding Train and 3Blue1Brown provided exactly that — visual, animated explanations that showed what neural networks actually *do* under the hood.

Watching activations move, weights adjust, and gradients cascade backward gave me a mental model that textbooks just don’t deliver. This was the point where machine learning stopped being a topic I was studying — it became something I *wanted* to build.

Learning PyTorch and Building My First Models

Once I understood the concepts, I moved on to PyTorch — and this is where everything clicked. I followed the freeCodeCamp PyTorch course, learning tensors, losses, optimizers, and training loops. It was the first time I felt like I was building neural networks instead of just learning about them.

Using PyTorch forced me to understand *why* things worked: – why tensor shapes matter – why the wrong learning rate causes collapse – what the optimizer is actually doing – how gradients, weights, and activations all interact Writing models line-by-line — not just running tutorial code — made everything feel real.

But the moment that fully inspired me was when I watched a breakdown of a shallow neural network and then recreated it myself. Seeing it train, predict, fail, and improve unlocked something in me: I realized that machine learning isn't magic — it's engineering.

I have detailed reports of the projects on my GitHub page for both my Housing Prices Predictor and my Diabetes Classification Model. The key take-away is this: by starting with small, focused projects you can set building blocks for larger, cooler ones.

Stay tuned.
